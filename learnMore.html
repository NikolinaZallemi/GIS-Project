Landslides are among the most hazardous natural instability processes, which globally lead enormous socio-economical losses and damage to property every year.
Shallow landslides and debris flows represent one of the major causes of destruction to structures and infrastructures,injured people and casualties in mountain and hilly regions: they are usually triggered by short and severe rainfalls. Shallow landslides generally involve small volumes, unlike debris flows.However, both can be extremely damaging due to their widespread spatial distribution across territories, rapid development and high velocity of propagation.
Landslide risk can be defined as the probability of the occurrence of a potentially damaging landslide in each area and for a defined period. Hazard assessment requires the estimation of the landslide magnitude, temporal frequency and spatial location .
The affectable area and recurrence time are complicated to predict because they are correlated to the complex natural processes that control the mass movement and the difficulty in raising historical landslide data respectively. Surely it could be easier to confine their predictive models to single slopes or very small areas for which detailed physical parameters and historical records can be found.
On the other hand, the spatial distribution of potentially unstable areas can be easier to predict by assessing the likelihood of landslide occurrence in a region on the basis of the local ground conditions.
Therefore, assessing landslide susceptibility represents the first step in evaluating landslide identification and mapping of areas, where slope instability occurred in the past. These areas are useful to predict the spatial location of future landslide initiation.

To start the problem from its genesis, we were given five affecting factors related to the landslides of our area of study. These features, selected to evaluate the occurrence of landslides and predict the future ones, were given in a certain data format:

The geo-environmental affecting factors were extracted from the DTM (Digital Terrain Model) Raster, which contains the elevation data of the terrain in a digital format. According to this raster, we rasterized all the above vector layers using as a burn-in value the distance and using the properties of DTM (CRS, extent, unit, pixel size):

Buffer vectors such as: roads, faults, rivers, show the area that is within a distance (defined in each map legend) from each map feature. The reason why we use buffer vectors is because we want to determine the areas which are near these map features to predict landslides surfaces. For example: Faults can be a factor that triggers landslides, but with buffer we can understand in what distance do the faults affect such surfaces. Hydrologic factors like rivers reflect the importance of ground water in the occurrence of landslides.

Another physical factor that determines a landslide risk is the “bedrock” or geology cover. For this reason, we decided to also add on this session, a map of geology of our area of interest. The data, represented as a vector, were downloaded from USGS. Click here.

The vector had a different Coordinate Reference System from our project, so we changed it from WGS 1984 Lambert Conformal Conic to WGS 84 / UTM zone 32N. From classifying these data and clipping them according to our group watershed, we concluded that the region’s geology cover consists of sedimentary rock units, as listed in the map’s legend. This is consequently showing that our study area is predisposed to be affected by landslides. This could be explained from the soft nature of sedimentary rocks. They are composed of many layers and their deep weathering weakens the rock making them unstable.

NDVI, as a remotely sensed data, is closely related to land degradation and landslide. This raster is used to mirror the vegetation density, as an influencing factor in susceptibility assessment studies.
NDVI is classified in 3 levels, with an 0.2 interval and 2 levels, one with values lower than 0 and one with values higher than 0.6. The higher the index value the denser the vegetation cover.

  • NDVI < 0 - In that part of the zone there is no vegetation. These values correspond to barren areas of rock.
  • NDVI 0-0.2 – Indicates that vegetation is in a stage before tillering.
  • NDVI 0.2-0.4 – Plants have resumed vegetation. Are considered moderate values that represent grassland.
  • NDVI 0.4-0.6 – Is a good value of vegetation and indicates healthy vegetation.
  • NDVI > 0.6 – It’s not always a good indicator, but it depends on the period when the area is monitored.

In our study area the index interval 0.4 - 0.6 occupies the largest percentage and the index >0.6 is almost absent. The territories with no vegetation are the ones extended near the inhabited areas.

Slope
From the dtm layer we extracted the Slope factor, determining the rising and falling surface, using degrees as unit.
The reason why we study this factor is because of its importance in defining the amount of mass movement during landslides.
We divided the slopes in 8 classes, as shown in the map, where one interval is 10o. 0 angle shows the areas with no slope, also called stable areas.
The areas with slope from 11o to 40o occupy the biggest percentage of the total area. Zones with slope bigger than 50 are almost not present. This fact shows that our area does not have large terrain fractures.
If we analyze the relation between landslides and slope angle, we notice that most of the landslides were observed for slopes > 20o.

Aspect
Aspect is an important factor because it has a crucial role in controlling factors such as rainfall intensity, soil moisture, weathering. In addition to this it controls the properties of the slope deposits material. It shows the direction the downhill slope is facing.
The layer is divided into 8 categories. From the map we can see that South and West-facing aspects dominate the classes.

Plan and Profile
One essential component of terrain instability is the landform, represented from two types of curvature such as plan and profile curvature. We are interested if the values of plan and profile curvatures are bigger, equal, or smaller than 0. According to this we classify the raster layers.
The phrase curvature relates to the scale of change of the slope and aspect rise. According to this relation we concluded that more landslides are present in the concave landforms. From the map we notice only small white dots of convex landforms.

During the data preprocessing, we focus on shallow landslides and exclude hard rocks. From the Slope layer we extract the No Landslide Zones (NLZ) layer by using the following expression in the raster calculator: “slope@1" < 20 OR " slope@1" > 70. NLZ correspond to pixels with value equal to 1.

As we can see we have a considerable number of small areas on the raster map, that is why we use the “Salt and Pepper” effect. This procedure removes raster polygons smaller than a provided threshold size (in pixels) and replaces them with the pixel value of the largest neighbor polygon.
We experimented by choosing three different thresholds until we got a clear representation of NLZ areas, with a threshold of 40.

As a further step we removed the NLZ, which were overlapping with the polygons of the Landslide Inventory layer. In both these layers we created a new field, in their attribute tables, named Hazard and assigned 0 to the NLZ and 1 to Landslide Inventory layer

Data preprocessing for susceptibility mapping
For the generation of our susceptibility map, we based the data preprocessing in a Machine Learning Algorithm.

‘Training points’ is the data we used to train the algorithm, in which the model relayed. On the other hand, the ‘Testing Points’ helped us validate the process of the algorithm’s training and optimize it for getting better results. We agreed to use a ratio 80/20 between training and testing points to train our model and a total of 3000 points.

During this stage of the project, we created random points inside the polygons of the merged vector layer, which contains the landslide and non-landslide areas. In this vector the values of Hazard equal to 0 correspond to non-landslide areas and Hazard equal to 1 correspond to landslide areas.
Moreover, we noticed that non-landslide areas are wider than the landslide ones. For this reason, we divided 2300 points on Hazard=0 and 700 on Hazard=1.

Why do we need the Landslide Susceptibility map?
As mentioned, landslides play a key role in hilly and mountainous environments, like our area, causing disasters and being a constant danger to the local population.
To prevent such catastrophe, it is important and necessary to map the territories susceptible to landslides. Information from the map can be used for land-use management.
To produce the Landslide Susceptibility model, the geological parameters were extracted from the Digital Terrain Model, with a resolution of 5m pixel size. This complex map contains data showing the risk of landslides in the area, divided in 4 classes or zones.
This distribution of landslides, in the 4 zones, is affected by the environmental factors used for the construction of the final map.

To get to this conclusion we used the Random Forest Classifier, in QGIS. As input for this feature, we used the Sampled Training Points and the Environmental Factors.

Exposure Assessment
Landslides cause serious consequences for the population of the region. For this reason, we have analyzed the population affected by the phenomenon showing the percentage of inhabitants in each susceptibility zone.
The data were taken from WorldPop Map website
After downloading the raster dataset, we reprojected and clipped the population raster according to our group watershed’s CRS. As Zones layer we used the resampled susceptibility raster map. This raster was resampled, because the resolution of the zones layer has to be lower than the input layer, which in this case is the Landslide Susceptibility map.

Population Exposure
We have shown each susceptibility class with different colors from low to very high.
The sum represents the value of population pixels that fall in the 4 classes.
In all, the diagram shows the exposure of the population, of our area of interest, to landslides. As it is shown most of the population, more than 90% of it, lives in areas with low risk of landslide. A very small percentage of them live in very high-risk exposure zone.

  • To remove small patches of pixels: Processing-GDAL-Raster Analysis-Sieve
  • To set negative values to null: r.null
  • To remove overlapping polygons: Processing- Vector Overlay- Difference
  • Select the percentage of polygons for training/testing points: Processing-Vector Selection- Random Selection
  • To create random points inside the polygons: Processing- Vector Creation-Random Points in layer bounds
  • To sample the environmental factors with the training and testing point layers: Point Sampling Tool plugin
  • To generate the Susceptibility Map: Processing-R-Landslide Susceptibility Mapping- R ModelMap for QGIS
  • To validate R output for the exposure assessment: Processing-Scripts-Accuracy Assessment and Sampling
  • To compute the population counts in each susceptibility class: Processing-Raster Analysis-Raster layer zonal statistics
